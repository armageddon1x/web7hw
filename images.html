Types of Stars

Stars are classified into different types, depending on their color and temperature. The different types of stars are O, B, A, F, G, K, and M stars. The O stars are the hottest and are blue-white in color. The M stars are the coolest and typically colored red.

Planets

Planets are large objects that orbit a star. Our solar system has eight (Pluto was considered a planet but the definition for a planet became more strigent a few years ago that disqualified Pluto). Each planet has its own properties that make it different from Earth, both visually and chemically. But each of them retains their own beauty as well.

In astronomy, the nomenclature of objects comes from the ancient myths of the ones who once observed them. The word "planet" comes from the Greek word for wanderer. Each of the planets has a name corresponding to a Greco-Roman god or goddess. Venus (Aphrodite) was the goddess of love, while Mars (Ares) was the god of war. Jupiter (Zeus) was the leader of the gods, and his grandfather was Uranus (Ouranos).Pluto was a brother of Zeus, and a god of the dead.

Some Weird Stuff

There are strange objects that are not well understood yet. For example, we have supernovas, which are stars that "explode" and release many larger elements out into space. While we understand a large part of supernova processes, there are still uncertainties in some parts of supernova creation.

When a supernova releases all its outer layers, only the dense neutron star core is left. A neutron star is the remnant of supernovae, after all the atoms have been compressed to a degree so that only the neutrons are left. Neutron stars are very dense and are also very small.

Black holes are a stage that go beyond neutron stars. Once a neutron star is too dense to sustain itself, it collapses into a point known as a black hole. Its gravity is so strong that even light cannot escape. Much research is needed to fully understand this strange and physics defying phenomenon.
